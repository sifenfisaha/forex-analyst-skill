# 09 — Psychology & Journaling (the real edge)

Strategy is maybe 20% of trading success. The other 80% is **executing the same strategy with discipline, trade after trade, especially after a loss.** This module is how the skill protects the user from themselves.

## The four account-killers (watch for these in the conversation)

| Killer | What it sounds like | The counter |
|---|---|---|
| **FOMO / boredom** | "There's nothing setting up but I want to trade something." | No setup = no trade. Screen time ≠ obligation. |
| **Revenge trading** | After a loss: "Let me make it back," sizing up, lower standards. | **Hard stop after 2 losses / −3%.** Walk away. |
| **Moving stops** | "I'll give it more room." | The stop is the invalidation. Moving it = no longer have a plan. |
| **Over-leverage** | Risking 3–5% "because I'm sure." | Fixed 0.5–1%. Certainty is the most expensive feeling in trading. |

When the user shows any of these, **name it gently but directly** and point back to the rule. Being the disciplined voice is part of the job — even when the user pushes.

## Pre-trade state check (30 seconds before risking money)

- Am I trading **the plan**, or a feeling?
- Did this setup **pass the scorecard** (all 4 gates + ≥ B)?
- Is my **risk fixed** at 0.5–1% with a logical stop?
- Am I **calm** — not chasing, not revenging, not euphoric?
- Is it a **valid session** with **news clear**?

If any answer is shaky → stand aside. The market opens again tomorrow.

## After a loss

A loss that followed the plan is a **good trade**. The strategy is built to be wrong often (≈1:3 RR means ~35–40% win rate is profitable). One loss means nothing. The danger isn't the loss — it's the *next* trade taken in frustration. Take a breath; the rules don't change because the last trade lost.

## After a win

Euphoria is as dangerous as frustration. A win doesn't mean size up or loosen criteria. Same process, same risk, next setup.

## Journaling — the only way to find your real edge

**You cannot improve what you don't measure.** The single highest-ROI habit in trading is logging every trade and reviewing weekly. Use `assets/trade-journal-template.csv`.

### Log for EVERY trade (win or loss):
- Date/time, pair, direction, session
- Setup grade (A+/B) and scorecard points
- Entry / stop / targets, planned RR
- The CRT/structure reason (sweep + MSS + array)
- Outcome (R won/lost), and **whether you followed the plan (Y/N)**
- Screenshot link (before + after)
- One-line note: what you did well / what to fix
- **Emotional state** at entry (calm / FOMO / revenge)

### The most important column: "Followed plan? Y/N"
Separate **process** from **outcome**:
- Followed plan + won → ✅ reinforce.
- Followed plan + lost → ✅ *still a good trade.* This is normal. Do not change anything.
- Broke plan + lost → ❌ the real problem. This is what to fix.
- Broke plan + won → ⚠️ **the most dangerous outcome** — it teaches a bad habit that will cost you later.

### Weekly review (every weekend)
1. Win rate, average RR, expectancy in R, net R for the week.
2. % of trades where you **followed the plan**. (Target: 95%+.)
3. Which **session / day / setup grade** is most profitable for you? Do more of that.
4. Where did losses cluster? (A pair? A session? Broke-plan trades?) Cut that.
5. Were any losses from **rule violations**? Those are free lessons — fix the behavior, not the strategy.

After ~30–50 logged trades you'll have *data*, not feelings, about your edge. That's when real improvement starts.

## The mindset in one line

> **Be a risk manager who happens to use CRT — not a CRT trader who hopes risk works out.** Protect the downside, execute the process, journal everything, and let probability do its job over a large sample.
